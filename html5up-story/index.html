María Alejandra Martínez Ortiz

Hola! I know I have a lot of names, but most people call me Alejandra (or Ale/Aleja). I am currently a lab manager at the Hartley Lab at New York University, where I study how children and adolescents learn and make decisions, and how these processes change as the brain develops. This fall, I will be starting my Ph.D in Psychology at Stanford University, where I will explore the nature of learning and memory in human infants with the Scaffolding of Cognition Team.

Where do I come from?

I was born and raised in Bogotá, a chaotically beautiful city in the Eastern Andes Mountains of Colombia. I grew up in a working-class neighborhood, surrounded by an incredibly supportive family. However, since neither my parents nor anyone in the generations before me had the opportunity to attend college, I navigated academia without a clear roadmap, facing debt and a lot of uncertainty. I changed careers twice before settling on psychology—first pursuing journalism and filmmaking, until I realized I would need personal funds to make movies; then switching to economics, hoping for a stable job in policy-making, until I realized I disliked most of the curriculum. In between these changes, I also worked as a customer service agent in a call center for an US based client, a job that, despite being absolutely devoid of soul, helped me immensely in improving my English speaking skills. My journey has been winding, and while I am grateful for every opportunity, I believe it would have been less stressful If I had more information about how academia works. This is why I am passionate about advising first-generation scholars on how to find and make the most of their academic and research opportunities. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you are an underrepresented scholar and would like to talk about this!

What is my academic trajectory?

I obtained my undergraduate degree in Psychology from the National University of Colombia, with an emphasis on Neurosciences. During this time, I worked with animal models to study the effects of early social isolation on the reinforcing properties of nicotine and social interaction during adolescence. I also had the opportunity to complete an internship at Purdue University through the UREP-C scholarship, where I studied the neurotoxic effects of chronic metal exposure in welders. Currently, my research at NYU focuses on the impact of Early Life Adversity (ELA) on learning and decision-making across development. I use various MRI techniques to examine how learning computations are associated with changes in brain structure and function.

Things I enjoy!

I enjoy watching movies, reading short stories, taking pictures, drinking coffee and learning new things. I particularly love stories that reimagine our universe by pushing the limits of science (sci-fi) or magic and mystery (fantasy/horror), although I don't really see much of a real difference between the two (I fundamentally think that science and magic are the same). I think a lot about cats, I observe them and learn from them constantly. I miss Hanna and Haku, my colombian cats with not so colombian names.
